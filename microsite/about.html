About the Great Smoky Mountains

The Great Smoky Mountains are a mountain range rising along the Tennesseeâ€“North Carolina border in the southeastern United States. They are a subrange of the Appalachian Mountains and form part of the Blue Ridge Physiographic Province.

Renowned for their diversity of plant and animal life, the beauty of their ancient mountains, and the remnants of Southern Appalachian mountain culture, the Smokies are a destination for nature lovers and adventurers alike.

As the most visited national park in the United States, the Great Smoky Mountains National Park offers endless opportunities for exploration and discovery.

The park was designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site because of its natural significance. Whether you are here to hike, photograph wildlife, or simply soak in the breathtaking landscapes, the Smokies welcome you to a world of wonder and tranquility.

Geography

The Great Smoky Mountains span across an area of approximately 800 square miles, with elevations ranging from 875 feet to 6,643 feet at the summit of Clingmans Dome, the highest point in the Smokies.

This diverse landscape includes lush forests, rushing streams, cascading waterfalls, and stunning vistas, offering visitors a variety of outdoor experiences.

History

The region has a rich cultural and historical significance, with evidence of human habitation dating back thousands of years. Native American tribes, including the Cherokee, inhabited these lands long before European settlers arrived.

In the 19th century, European settlers began to establish communities in the area, relying on logging, farming, and mining for their livelihoods.

The establishment of the Great Smoky Mountains National Park in 1934 preserved this natural and cultural heritage for future generations to enjoy.

Wildlife

The Great Smoky Mountains are renowned for their incredible biodiversity, hosting over 19,000 documented species, including black bears, white-tailed deer, wild turkeys, and a myriad of bird species.

The park is also home to elusive creatures such as the synchronous fireflies, whose mesmerizing light displays attract visitors from around the world.

Recreation

Visitors to the Great Smoky Mountains National Park can enjoy a wide range of outdoor activities, including hiking, camping, fishing, wildlife watching, and scenic drives along the park's picturesque roads.

The park boasts over 800 miles of maintained trails, catering to hikers of all skill levels. Popular trails include the Appalachian Trail, Alum Cave Trail, and Laurel Falls Trail.

Cultural Heritage

Exploring the Great Smoky Mountains offers insights into the region's rich cultural heritage, including preserved historic structures such as cabins, churches, and grist mills.

Rangers and volunteers provide educational programs and guided tours, allowing visitors to learn about the traditions, customs, and stories of the people who once called these mountains home.
